Survival Analysis: Veteran Lung Cancer Patients

Author

Julian Chung

1 📘 Introduction

This dataset originates from a clinical trial conducted by the Veterans Administration in the United States to compare the effectiveness of two chemotherapy regimens in treating advanced, inoperable lung cancer. Patients were randomized to receive either a standard treatment or a test treatment (an experimental chemotherapy protocol), and followed until death or censoring. The primary outcome was overall survival, measured in days from treatment initiation.

The dataset contains information on:

Treatment group (Standard vs Test),

Cell type (squamous, small cell, adenocarcinoma, or large cell),

Survival time and status,

Karnofsky performance score (a proxy for baseline health),

Time from diagnosis to study entry,

Patient age, and

Number of prior treatments received.

The data is widely used in survival analysis teaching due to its realistic structure, meaningful clinical context, and manageable size.

4.0.1 🧠 Interpretation

The Kaplan-Meier plot shows survival curves for patients receiving standard vs test treatment.

Interpretation of Graph Elements

X-Axis: Time (days)

The X-axis shows the time since enrollment in days. The furthest value to the right (~1000 days) marks the maximum observed survival time in the dataset. Shorter durations towards the left reflect early mortality events.

Y-Axis: Survival Probability

The Y-Axis is the Kaplan-Meier estimate of the probability that a patient survives beyond a certain timepoint. The axis starts at 1 (100% survival at time 0), and decreases with each event (death).

Curves (Blue = Standard, Orange = Test)

Each step downward represents a death. Tick marks would represent a censored individual (few in this dataset).

Shaded Area: 95% Confidence Interval

Around each curve is a shaded band indicating uncertainty in the estimate. Wider intervals to the right of the chart reflect fewer patients remaining and hence more variability in survival probability.

p = 0.93

The log-rank test result tests the null hypothesis: there is no difference in survival between the groups. The p-value of 0.93 provides strong evidence against rejecting the H0. In other words, there is no statistically significant difference in survival between the Standard and Test treatments. If the curves had diverged strongly at some point, the P-value would be lower.

“Number at Risk” Table

This shows how many participatns in each treatment group are still alive and uncensored at various timepoints (0, 250, 500, 750, 1000). As time progresses the number declines due to deaths or censoring. Few participants remain beyond 500 days, which is why the Confidence Intervals widen later in the graph.

  • The p-value is from a log-rank test (H₀: no difference in survival curves).
  • The shaded areas represent 95% confidence intervals.
  • The risk table shows the number of patients still at risk at each time point.

Visually, if the curves separate early and remain apart, it suggests a potential treatment effect.

Interpretation of the Cox Model Results

The Cox proportional hazards model found no statistically significant difference in survival between the Control and Intervention groups (HR = 1.02, 95% CI: 0.71–1.45, p = 0.922). The hazard ratio close to 1 suggests that the treatment had no appreciable effect on the risk of death. Additionally, the wide confidence interval includes both potential harm and benefit, indicating uncertainty in the estimate. The concordance statistic (0.525) implies the model has poor discriminative ability, performing only slightly better than random chance. Overall, the analysis provides no evidence that the intervention improves survival outcomes in this dataset.

Why use ggadjustedcurves() for this plot

For plotting survival curves adjusted for covariates from a Cox Proportional Hazards model, the ggadjustedcurves() function from the survminer package offers one of the most direct and statistically appropriate approaches—particularly as model complexity increases.

Statistical validity: ggadjustedcurves() uses the fitted cox_model to compute adjusted survival probabilities for the variable of interest (in this case, trt). When method = "average" is specified (the default when covariates are present), it returns marginal estimates—appropriate for interpreting average treatment effects across the population. In our simpler model (~ trt), it effectively plots the model-predicted survival curve for each treatment group.

Robustness: This function is purpose-built for adjusted curve plotting and avoids many of the pitfalls seen with manually passing newdata to survfit() and plotting with ggsurvplot(), such as misaligned strata or legend issues. It correctly associates the model, the data, and the plot elements, including the risk table.

Simplicity: It eliminates the need for manually constructing a new_trt data frame or calling survfit() separately, resulting in cleaner, more reproducible code—particularly useful in collaborative or long-term projects.

The Cox-adjusted survival curves for the Control and Intervention groups are almost perfectly overlapping, indicating no meaningful difference in predicted survival probabilities between the two treatment arms. This is consistent with the hazard ratio from the Cox model, which was very close to 1, and not statistically significant. Visually, the indistinguishability of the curves reinforces the conclusion that the treatment had no observable impact on survival in this dataset.

Why do the lines overlap?

The survival curves for the Control and Intervention groups overlap almost entirely in this plot. This is because the Cox proportional hazards model found no significant difference between the two groups in terms of survival (hazard ratio ≈ 1, p = 0.922). As a result, the model-predicted curves are nearly identical. The faint visibility of the Control group line is due to it being directly behind the Intervention group line.

  • trt p = 0.06: This is slightly above the conventional threshold of 0.05. It suggest no strong evidence of violation of the proportional hazards (PH) assumptions for treatment.
  • GLOBAL p = 0.06: This tests the model overall. Not statistically significant so we do not reject the PH assumptions.

Porportional Hazards Assumption

The proportional hazards assumption was assessed using Schoenfeld residuals via the cox.zph() function. The test for the treatment variable (trt) yielded a chi-square statistic of 3.54 with a p-value of 0.06, and the global test returned the same values (χ² = 3.54, df = 1, p = 0.06).

Although the p-values are slightly above the conventional significance threshold of 0.05, they do not provide sufficient evidence to reject the null hypothesis that the proportional hazards assumption holds. As such, the Cox proportional hazards model is considered appropriate for this analysis, though the borderline result suggests caution and may warrant further inspection in more complex models.

6.2 Schoenfeld Residuals Plot

Code 
plot(cox.zph(cox_model))
What the Visual Diagnostic Shows

The y-axis: Scaled Schoenfeld residuals for trt. If the proportional hazards assumption holds, these residuals should fluctuate randomly around zero.

The x-axis: Time (or transformed time).

The line: A smoothed spline fit (typically a loess curve) showing the trend of residuals over time.

The confidence band: Helps you assess whether the trend significantly deviates from horizontal (i.e., no time-dependent effect).

A flat line (slope ≈ 0): Suggests the hazard ratio for trt does not change over time — good!

A wavy or sloped line: Suggests potential violation of the proportional hazards assumption — this would back up a significant p-value.

x-axis (Time): Time since baseline (likely in days, from your dataset).

y-axis (Beta(t) for trt): Time-varying estimate of the log hazard ratio for the treatment group (trt), based on Schoenfeld residuals.

The solid black line: A smoothed curve showing how the estimated coefficient for trt might change over time.

Dashed lines: Approximate 95% confidence bands around that smoothed estimate.

Open circles: Residuals for each individual event — they don’t need to follow a pattern, but their distribution can hint at model fit.

If the solid line is roughly flat and within the dashed lines, the effect of treatment does not change with time, and the PH assumption is reasonably upheld.

If the solid line drifts noticeably over time, or wanders outside the dashed lines, that’s evidence that the treatment effect may change — a violation of the PH assumption.

In our case, the line is gently curved, but still well within the dashed boundaries. There’s no strong time trend — the log hazard ratio is hovering around zero and doesn’t appear to increase or decrease dramatically.

Schoenfeld Residuals Interpretation

To assess whether the proportional hazards (PH) assumption holds for the treatment variable (trt), we examined Schoenfeld residuals using both statistical and graphical diagnostics. The global test for proportionality yielded a p-value of 0.06, indicating no statistically significant violation of the PH assumption at the conventional α = 0.05 level, though it approaches significance. The smoothed residual plot for trt shows a relatively stable trend over time, with the estimated log hazard ratio remaining within the 95% confidence bands across the follow-up period. 6.5 Additional Covariates

7 🔀 Multivariable Cox Regression

To build on the univariable analysis of treatment effects, we next fit a multivariable Cox proportional hazards model that incorporates additional patient-level covariates. This allows us to estimate the effect of treatment on survival while adjusting for other potential prognostic factors, such as age and prior treatment history. By doing so, we account for potential confounding and improve the model’s explanatory power.

What is a multivariable Cox model and why are we using it?

In a univariable Cox model, we examine the effect of one predictor at a time on the hazard of an event (e.g., death). While this can be useful for preliminary exploration, it doesn’t account for the influence of other variables that might confound or modify the relationship.

A multivariable Cox proportional hazards model includes multiple covariates simultaneously. This allows us to: - Estimate the adjusted effect of each variable while controlling for the others, - Improve model accuracy and interpretability, - Detect whether the apparent effect of one variable (e.g., treatment) persists after adjusting for others (e.g., age, comorbidities), - Explore potential interactions or effect modification.

In this section, we’ll first add a continuous covariate (age) and then a binary variable (prior treatment) to the model. We’ll also assess whether the functional form of continuous variables is appropriate and ensure that model assumptions still hold.

::: {callout-tip title=“Why not use logistic regression?”} Logistic regression ignores time-to-event and censoring. When working with survival data, Cox models are preferred as they preserve the time structure and allow for censored cases. :::

7.1 📥 Add Covariates (Age, Prior Treatment)

To expand the model beyond treatment alone, we now include additional patient characteristics: age (a continuous variable) and prior (indicating whether the patient received prior therapy, a binary variable). Including these covariates allows us to better account for baseline differences between patients, assess their independent associations with survival, and evaluate how these variables may influence the effect of treatment.

Why include more covariates?

Adding additional covariates serves several important purposes in survival analysis:

  • Control for confounding: Variables like age and prior treatment may influence both survival and the likelihood of receiving a particular treatment. Including them helps isolate the true effect of treatment.
  • Improve model accuracy: Covariates that predict survival can increase the model’s explanatory power and reduce residual variation.
  • Uncover independent predictors: Some variables (like prior therapy) may have a meaningful relationship with survival, regardless of treatment.
  • Assess effect modification: Once multiple variables are included, we can test for interactions — for example, whether the effect of treatment depends on age or prior treatment status.

We’ll begin by examining age as a continuous covariate (with extra attention to its functional form), followed by prior as a binary covariate. We’ll then combine them in a multivariable Cox model.

Interpreting the Cox Model Output

Understanding the coxph() output is essential for interpreting survival models. Here’s a breakdown of the most common components you’ll encounter in both univariable and multivariable Cox regressions:

🔹 coef
This is the log hazard ratio — the raw estimate from the model. It represents the change in the log hazard of the event (e.g., death) associated with a one-unit increase in the covariate (for continuous variables) or relative to a reference group (for categorical variables).

  • Positive coef → increased hazard (i.e., higher risk)
  • Negative coef → decreased hazard (i.e., protective)
  • A coef close to 0 suggests little to no effect

🧠 Note: Log hazards are difficult to interpret directly, so we usually exponentiate them.

🔹 exp(coef)
This is the hazard ratio (HR) — the exponentiated coef, which expresses effect size more intuitively.

  • exp(coef) > 1 → Increased hazard
  • exp(coef) < 1 → Decreased hazard
  • exp(coef) = 1 → No effect

🔎 Example: exp(coef) = 1.20 means a 20% higher hazard.
exp(coef) = 0.80 means a 20% lower hazard.

🔹 se(coef)
The standard error of the coefficient — reflects how much uncertainty surrounds the estimate.

  • Smaller values = more precise estimates
  • Larger values = more uncertainty (due to small samples or noisy data)

🔹 z and Pr(>|z|)
- z is the Z-statistic, calculated as coef / se(coef)
- Pr(>|z|) is the p-value from the Wald test for each coefficient

This tests the null hypothesis that the covariate has no effect (coef = 0).

  • p < 0.05 → Statistically significant
  • p > 0.05 → Not statistically significant

📊 Model-Level Statistics

🔸 Concordance
- Measures the model’s ability to correctly rank survival times (similar to AUC for classification)
- Ranges from 0.5 (random) to 1.0 (perfect)
- In clinical data, values of 0.6–0.75 are typical

🔸 Likelihood Ratio Test
- Compares the full model to a null (intercept-only) model
- Tests overall model fit
- p < 0.05 = your model significantly improves fit

🔸 Wald Test
- Aggregates the individual Wald tests across all covariates
- Tests whether any coefficients differ significantly from 0

🔸 Score (Logrank) Test
- Rank-based test comparing observed vs expected survival
- Can be slightly more sensitive in small samples
- Often used as an overall model check

✅ In practice: Use all three global tests and the concordance to evaluate how well the model explains variation in survival. If all are non-significant, the model may lack predictive utility.

Interpreting Schoenfeld Residuals

Schoenfeld residuals are used to test the Proportional Hazards (PH) assumption in Cox models — that the effect of each covariate remains constant over time.

How to interpret the output:

  • Each row (e.g., trt, age) gives a test of proportionality for that specific variable.
  • The GLOBAL row tests all covariates together.

What the numbers mean:

  • chisq is the test statistic (larger values = more evidence of time-varying effects).
  • p is the p-value testing null hypothesis: proportional hazards holds.

Rule of thumb: - p < 0.05 → PH assumption violated (bad) - p > 0.05 → PH assumption holds (good)

🧠 Note: Slight violations (e.g., p = 0.055) might not be a problem in practice — but major ones (e.g., p = 0.01) should be addressed.

Understanding Spline and Non-Linear Transformations in Survival Models

Why model transformations at all?
The Cox Proportional Hazards model assumes that each covariate has a linear relationship with the log hazard. For continuous variables like age, this means that each one-unit increase is expected to have the same multiplicative effect on the hazard — regardless of whether someone is 40 or 80 years old. This is often unrealistic.

To fix this, we apply non-linear transformations to more accurately capture the shape of the relationship between a variable (like age) and the outcome (e.g., death).

What are spline transformations?
Splines are a flexible way to model non-linear relationships without explicitly specifying the form of the curve. They break a variable into segments using predefined cut points called knots, and fit separate curves (usually polynomials) between them, ensuring smoothness at the joins.

They allow the model to “bend” as needed — making them perfect for capturing complex patterns in the data.

Common types of transformations:

• Restricted Cubic Splines (Natural Splines):
This is the most common spline in Cox models. It creates piecewise cubic polynomials between knots, but constrains the ends to be linear — which prevents overfitting at the tails.

  • ✅ Best for flexible yet stable curve fitting
  • 📌 Recommended when you suspect non-linearity but don’t want to manually test transformations

• Quadratic or Polynomial Terms (e.g., age + I(age^2)):
Adds a squared (or cubic) term to capture curvature.

  • ✅ Simple to interpret
  • ⚠️ Risky at the extremes — can overfit or behave erratically

• Log Transformations (e.g., log(age)):
Used when the effect decelerates — i.e., rapid effect early on that flattens with increasing value.

  • ✅ Great when data is skewed
  • ⚠️ Doesn’t work if the variable contains 0s or negatives

• Categorization (e.g., age groups):
Turns a continuous variable into bins (e.g., under 50, 50–65, over 65).

  • ✅ Easy to explain
  • ⚠️ Loses statistical power and may mislead if cutoffs are arbitrary

When should you use each?

Transformation Use when…
Splines (ns()) You want a smooth, data-driven relationship
Quadratic term You suspect a simple “U-shaped” or curvilinear trend
Log transform The variable increases rapidly then levels off
Categories Clinical guidelines or reporting require it

How many knots do I use for splines?
A good rule of thumb (Harrell, 2015):

  • 3 knots: small datasets (n < 100)
  • 4–5 knots: medium datasets (n ~ 100–300)
  • 5–7 knots: large datasets

Knots are often placed at percentiles of the variable — e.g., 5th, 35th, 65th, 95th percentile.

Final tip: Always re-check Schoenfeld residuals and Martingale residuals after applying any transformation.

Understanding ANOVA in Cox Regression

In the context of Cox proportional hazards models, ANOVA isn’t used in the traditional sense (like comparing means). Instead, it’s a way of comparing nested models using their log-likelihoods.

This is typically done using the Likelihood Ratio Test (LRT), which answers the question:

“Does the more complex model fit significantly better than the simpler one?”

7.3.1 🔍 What the Output Shows:

  • Model 1: The simpler model (fewer terms or more constrained terms)
  • Model 2: The more complex model (extra variables or flexible terms, e.g., splines)
  • Log-likelihoods: How well each model fits the data (less negative = better)
  • Chisq: Computed as 2 × (LL₂ − LL₁)
  • Degrees of freedom: Difference in number of parameters
  • p-value: Significance of improvement in fit

7.3.2 ✅ How to Interpret:

  • p < 0.05 → The complex model fits significantly better → keep it
  • p ≥ 0.05 → No meaningful improvement → stick with the simpler model

This approach is especially useful for: - Deciding whether to model continuous variables non-linearly (e.g., with splines) - Evaluating whether new covariates add predictive value - Performing stepwise model refinement

📦 Takeaway:
Use ANOVA/LRT in Cox regression to guide decisions about model complexity vs parsimony, balancing better fit with interpretability.

How to Interpret Term Plots in Cox Models

Term plots visualize the partial effect of each covariate in a fitted model while holding other variables constant. They are especially helpful for interpreting transformations like splines.

🔹 What is a term plot?
A term plot shows how the log hazard (on the y-axis) changes with a covariate (on the x-axis), after accounting for all other variables in the model.

  • The blue line is the estimated partial effect of that variable.
  • The dashed orange lines show the 95% confidence interval.
  • The interpretation depends on the type of variable:

🔸 Continuous Variables (e.g., Age):

  • For linear terms, the blue line should be roughly straight.
  • For splines or transformed terms:
    • A curved blue line indicates a non-linear relationship.
    • Areas where the confidence band does not include 0 suggest a statistically significant effect on the log hazard.
    • Patterns like U-shapes, inflection points, or steep climbs reveal how risk evolves across the covariate range.

🔸 Binary Variables (e.g., Treatment, Prior Therapy):

  • These are shown as two horizontal segments:
    • One for each group (e.g., Control = 0, Intervention = 1).
    • The difference between the two shows the log hazard shift due to that variable.
  • If the confidence intervals include zero, there’s no strong evidence that the variable affects the hazard.

✅ Best Uses:

  • Confirming that spline-transformed terms behave as expected.
  • Checking if a linear term should be transformed (e.g., age might be better modeled with splines).
  • Double-checking whether binary variables have any meaningful effect.

🧠 Tip: Term plots don’t replace statistical tests, but they visualize model estimates clearly and intuitively.
