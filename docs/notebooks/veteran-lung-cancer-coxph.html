Survival Analysis of Veterans with Advanced Lung Cancer: A Cox Proportional Hazards Approach

Author

Julian Chung

1 Introduction

This report presents a survival analysis of patients with advanced, inoperable lung cancer who participated in a randomized clinical trial conducted by the Veterans Administration in the United States. The objective of the study was to compare the effectiveness of a standard chemotherapy protocol versus a test (experimental) treatment in prolonging patient survival.

Participants were followed until death or censoring, with overall survival time (in days from treatment initiation) as the primary outcome. This dataset, originally introduced in The Statistical Analysis of Failure Time Data by Kalbfleisch and Prentice (1980), has become a foundational paper in the teaching survival analysis due to its manageable size, clear structure, and strong clinical relevance.

This analysis explores survival outcomes using both Kaplan-Meier estimation and Cox proportional hazards modeling, followed by diagnostic checks to assess model assumptions and potential improvements.

3 Exploratory Survival Analysis

Before proceeding with formal regression modeling, we begin with a non-parametric exploration of the survival data using the Kaplan-Meier estimator. This allows us to visualize survival patterns and group differences without making assumptions about the underlying hazard function.

Parametric vs Non-Parametric Methods in Survival Analysis

Non-parametric methods, like the Kaplan-Meier estimator and log-rank test, do not assume a specific mathematical form for the distribution of survival times. Instead, they rely solely on the observed data to estimate survival probabilities. These approaches are ideal for exploratory analysis, detecting group differences, and visualizing survival trends without imposing structural assumptions.

Parametric methods, on the other hand, assume that survival times follow a known probability distribution (e.g., exponential, Weibull). This allows for more efficient estimation and extrapolation — but only if the model fits well. They are powerful for making predictions and simulating outcomes but can be misleading if the chosen distribution doesn’t reflect the true underlying process.

Semi-parametric methods, like the Cox proportional hazards model, strike a balance. The Cox model doesn’t assume a specific baseline hazard function (non-parametric), but it does assume a parametric relationship between covariates and the log hazard (i.e., proportional hazards). This makes it one of the most flexible and widely used tools in survival analysis.

Use non-parametric approaches for initial exploration or when we want minimal assumptions.
Use (semi-)parametric models when we want to assess covariate effects or make adjusted predictions.

Why Start with a Kaplan-Meier Curve?

The Kaplan-Meier (KM) curve is a fundamental starting point in survival analysis because it allows us to:

  • Visualize survival patterns over time,
  • Compare groups (e.g., treatment vs control) using log-rank tests,
  • And understand the overall shape and drop-off of survival before making model-based assumptions.

The KM method is non-parametric, which means it does not assume any specific distribution for survival times. It simply calculates the probability of surviving past a given time point based on the observed data.

This makes it ideal as a descriptive tool and as a way to check for potential group differences before moving on to more complex models.

By contrast, the Cox proportional hazards model is a semi-parametric model. It assumes: - A baseline hazard function (unspecified), - And that covariates have a multiplicative effect on the hazard over time.

This adds power and flexibility, but also introduces assumptions — like the proportional hazards assumption — that must be checked.

So we start with KM to understand the data and get a rough idea of group differences — without assuming too much — before fitting a Cox model.

3.2 Log-Rank Test

The log-rank test is a statistical test used to compare the survival distributions of two or more groups. It assesses whether there are significant differences in survival times between the groups, based on the observed and expected number of events (deaths) at each time point.

What is the Log-Rank Test?

The log-rank test is a non-parametric statistical test used to compare the survival distributions of two or more independent groups. It evaluates the null hypothesis that there is no difference in the hazard rates between the groups over time—that is, the survival curves are statistically indistinguishable.

The test works by comparing the observed number of events (e.g., deaths) in each group to the expected number of events under the assumption that all groups share the same survival function. At each time point where an event occurs, the log-rank test calculates the difference between the observed and expected events across groups, summing these differences over time to produce a test statistic.

This statistic approximately follows a chi-squared distribution, and the resulting p-value indicates whether the observed differences are likely due to chance. A small p-value (typically < 0.05) suggests a statistically significant difference in survival between groups.

In the context of Cox proportional hazards analysis, the log-rank test is often used before fitting a Cox model to determine whether a survival difference exists between categorical groups (e.g., treatment arms). While the log-rank test is limited to univariate group comparisons and assumes proportional hazards, it provides a simple and intuitive starting point for exploring potential treatment effects or group differences in survival.

Understanding Chi-Square and Degrees of Freedom

The chi-square (χ²) statistic is a measure of how much the observed data deviate from what we would expect under a specific statistical model — in this case, the assumption that survival curves are the same across groups.

In tests like the log-rank test or Cox model diagnostics, the chi-square statistic is calculated by comparing the observed vs expected number of events (deaths) for each group at every time point. The greater the deviation between these values, the larger the chi-square statistic becomes.

The degrees of freedom (df) represent the number of independent comparisons being made. For example: - If you’re comparing two groups, there’s only one degree of freedom (df = 1). - If you’re comparing k groups, df = k – 1.

Once the chi-square statistic and degrees of freedom are known, we can compute a p-value — the probability of observing such a deviation (or more extreme) if the null hypothesis were true.

  • Small p-value (e.g., < 0.05): Strong evidence against the null hypothesis → suggests a difference between groups.
  • Large p-value (e.g., > 0.05): No strong evidence → we fail to reject the null hypothesis.

In short:
Chi-square quantifies the discrepancy.
Degrees of freedom tell us how many comparisons we’re making.
P-value tells us whether the discrepancy is statistically meaningful.

Interpretation of the Log-Rank Test Results

A log-rank test was performed to compare survival distributions between the control and intervention treatment groups. The test yielded a chi-square statistic of 0.008 with 1 degree of freedom, resulting in a p-value of 0.93. This indicates no statistically significant difference in survival between the two treatment arms. The observed and expected number of events (deaths) were nearly identical in both groups, further supporting the conclusion that survival outcomes did not differ meaningfully between treatments in this sample.

However, the log-rank test is a univariate method — it does not account for covariates that may confound or modify treatment effects (e.g., age, prior therapy). Additionally, it assumes proportional hazards and constant hazard differences over time. These limitations justify the use of a Cox proportional hazards model, which allows for multivariable adjustment and more nuanced interpretation of the treatment effect in the context of patient characteristics.

Why Continue Cox Modeling After a Non-Significant Log-Rank Test?

Even if the Kaplan-Meier curve and log-rank test show no statistically significant difference between groups, there are several valid and important reasons to continue with a Cox proportional hazards model:

The Kaplan-Meier and log-rank test are unadjusted.
They assess only one variable at a time and do not control for confounders like age, prior treatment, or performance score. A Cox model allows you to adjust for these and may reveal effects that were previously masked.

Cox regression provides more than just a p-value.
Unlike the log-rank test, the Cox model estimates hazard ratios (HRs) and their confidence intervals, which are essential for understanding the magnitude and direction of effects — even if not statistically significant.

Exploratory analysis and skill building are valuable.
In educational or preliminary research contexts, building and diagnosing models provides critical insight. You may discover interesting patterns or generate hypotheses for future studies.

Diagnostics may reveal model violations or improvements.
Even in the absence of significant treatment effects, diagnostic tools (like residual plots or proportional hazards tests) can highlight non-linear effects, time-varying hazards, or outliers that inform better modelling strategies.

Bottom line: The absence of significance in univariate survival tests does not mean “stop here.” A Cox model is not just a hypothesis test — it’s a framework for adjusted inference, deeper insight, and model-based exploration.

3.3 Why Proceed with the Cox Model?

While the Kaplan-Meier plot and log-rank test indicate no statistically significant difference in survival between treatment arms, these methods do not account for other variables that may affect outcomes. The Cox proportional hazards model allows us to:

  • Adjust for confounding covariates (e.g., age, prior treatment)
  • Estimate hazard ratios for each variable
  • Explore possible interactions or time-varying effects

Thus, we proceed with a Cox model to assess whether treatment effects become more apparent after adjusting for relevant clinical factors.

4 Cox Regression Models

The Cox proportional hazards model is a regression method for analysing time-to-event data. Unlike the Kaplan-Meier estimator, which provides a non-parametric, unadjusted view of survival, the Cox model allows us to examine the effect of multiple covariates on the hazard of death.

This model assumes that covariates have a multiplicative and time-constant effect on the hazard (the proportional hazards assumption). By estimating hazard ratios, we can assess whether factors such as treatment type, age, or prior therapy are independently associated with better or worse survival outcomes.

We begin with a simple model including only treatment group, and progressively build toward a multivariable model incorporating continuous and categorical variables. Each model is followed by diagnostic checks to assess assumptions and guide model refinement.

Understanding the Cox Proportional Hazards Model

The Cox proportional hazards model (often just called the Cox model) is a cornerstone of survival analysis, allowing us to investigate how multiple variables influence the risk (hazard) of an event over time — without needing to specify the baseline hazard function.

What is a Hazard?
The hazard is the instantaneous risk of the event (e.g., death) at a given time, given that the individual has survived up to that point. The Cox model expresses this as:

h(t | X) = h₀(t) × exp(β₁X₁ + β₂X₂ + … + βₖXₖ)

Where: - h(t | X) is the hazard at time t for a subject with covariates X, - h₀(t) is the baseline hazard (unspecified), - β are the coefficients estimating the effect of each covariate.

Why Use the Cox Model Instead of Kaplan-Meier?

In practice, it’s common to start with a Kaplan-Meier plot to visualize survival, then use a Cox model to quantify the effect of predictors and control for confounding.

What Does “Proportional Hazards” Mean?
The Cox model assumes that the hazard ratio between two individuals is constant over time.
For example, if a treatment reduces the hazard by 30%, it is assumed to reduce it by 30% at every point in time. This is the proportional hazards assumption, and it must be checked using diagnostic tools like Schoenfeld residuals.

Model Diagnostics

Before interpreting the results of a Cox proportional hazards model, it’s important to ensure that the model’s assumptions are met. The two key assumptions are:

  • The proportional hazards (PH) assumption: The effect of each covariate on the hazard is constant over time.

  • Linearity of continuous covariates: Continuous covariates are assumed to have a linear relationship with the log hazard.

Violations of these assumptions can undermine the model’s predictive utility and lead to misleading conclusions.

In this section, we use the following diagnostic tools to assess model adequacy:

  • Schoenfeld residuals to test the PH assumption.

  • Martingale residuals to check for non-linearity and outliers.

  • Functional form checks, particularly for continuous covariates like age.

  • Where needed, we apply transformations (e.g., natural splines) to better capture nonlinear effects.

These checks help ensure that the model remains a sound and interpretable framework for estimating covariate effects on survival.

Checking the Proportional Hazards Assumption

A central assumption of the Cox proportional hazards model is that hazard ratios remain constant over time — meaning the relative risk between any two individuals does not change as time progresses. This is known as the proportional hazards (PH) assumption, and verifying it is crucial before interpreting model results.

Schoenfeld Residuals

To assess the PH assumption, we use Schoenfeld residuals, which evaluate whether covariate effects vary over time. If residuals show a systematic pattern against time, it may indicate a violation of the assumption.

We check this in two ways:

  • Statistical tests: The cox.zph() function provides both a global test and tests for each covariate. A p-value < 0.05 suggests evidence against the PH assumption.

  • Visual inspection: Plots of scaled Schoenfeld residuals include a smoothed line and 95% confidence bands. Systematic trends or large deviations from zero may indicate time-varying effects.

If either test indicates a violation, we may need to adjust the model using time-varying covariates, stratification, or alternative approaches.

Understanding Chi-Squared and p-values in PH Assumption Testing

When checking the proportional hazards (PH) assumption in a Cox model using cox.zph(), each covariate — and the model as a whole — is tested for time-dependent effects.

  • Chi-squared statistic: Measures how much the residuals deviate from what would be expected if the covariate had a constant effect over time (i.e., proportional hazards). A larger value suggests a stronger departure from proportionality.

  • Degrees of freedom (df): Typically 1 for individual covariates, and equal to the number of covariates in the global test.

  • p-value: Quantifies the probability that the observed chi-squared value (or more extreme) could occur by chance if the PH assumption holds.

How to interpret: - p > 0.05: No significant evidence of time-varying effects — PH assumption likely holds. - p < 0.05: Suggests the effect of the covariate may change over time — a potential violation of the PH assumption.

Use both statistical tests and residual plots for a more robust assessment. A borderline p-value (e.g., 0.06) should be interpreted with caution and always viewed in conjunction with the corresponding plot.

Understanding Martingale Residuals

Martingale residuals are used to assess whether the functional form of a continuous covariate is correctly specified in a Cox proportional hazards model.

What are they?
Martingale residuals represent the difference between the observed number of events and the expected number of events under the fitted model for each subject.
They range from –∞ to +1 and are asymmetrically distributed.

Why do we use them?
To check the linearity assumption: the Cox model assumes that the covariate has a linear effect on the log hazard. If this is not true (e.g. the effect increases or decreases non-linearly), the model may misestimate risk.

What should you look for in the plot?
You typically plot Martingale residuals against a continuous covariate (e.g. age). Then:

  • A random scatter around the horizontal line at 0 suggests linearity is reasonable.
  • A systematic curve (e.g., U-shaped, S-shaped) suggests non-linearity and the need for transformations (e.g., log(age), age², splines).

Practical Tip:
Martingale residuals are not symmetric, so smooth trends can be hard to detect visually. It’s a good idea to pair this check with ggcoxfunctional() or use splines to model nonlinear effects more flexibly.

Why Add Covariates to a Cox Model?

Including additional covariates in a Cox proportional hazards model serves several purposes:

  • Control for confounding: Variables such as age or prior treatment may influence both the outcome and the likelihood of receiving a particular treatment. Adjusting for them helps isolate the effect of the treatment itself.

  • Improve model accuracy: Predictive covariates enhance the model’s explanatory power and reduce residual variation.

  • Estimate independent effects: Including multiple variables allows us to assess their individual contributions to survival outcomes, independent of each other.

  • Explore effect modification: With covariates in the model, we can investigate interactions — for example, whether the effect of treatment varies by age or prior therapy.

When selecting covariates, decisions should be guided by clinical relevance and theoretical grounding, not just statistical significance.

4.2 Multivariable Models

To build on the univariable analysis of treatment effects, we next fit a multivariable Cox proportional hazards model that incorporates additional patient-level covariates. This allows us to estimate the effect of treatment on survival while adjusting for other potential prognostic factors, such as age and prior treatment history. By doing so, we account for potential confounding and improve the model’s explanatory power.

What is a multivariable Cox model and why are we using it?

In a univariable Cox model, we examine the effect of one predictor at a time on the hazard of an event (e.g., death). While this can be useful for preliminary exploration, it doesn’t account for the influence of other variables that might confound or modify the relationship.

A multivariable Cox proportional hazards model includes multiple covariates simultaneously. This allows us to: - Estimate the adjusted effect of each variable while controlling for the others, - Improve model accuracy and interpretability, - Detect whether the apparent effect of one variable (e.g., treatment) persists after adjusting for others (e.g., age, comorbidities), - Explore potential interactions or effect modification.

In this section, we’ll first add a continuous covariate (age) and then a binary variable (prior treatment) to the model. We’ll also assess whether the functional form of continuous variables is appropriate and ensure that model assumptions still hold.

Why not use logistic regression?

Logistic regression ignores time-to-event and censoring. When working with survival data, Cox models are preferred as they preserve the time structure and allow for censored cases.

Interpreting the Cox Model Output

Understanding the coxph() output is essential for interpreting survival models. Here’s a breakdown of the most common components you’ll encounter in both univariable and multivariable Cox regressions:

🔹 coef
This is the log hazard ratio — the raw estimate from the model. It represents the change in the log hazard of the event (e.g., death) associated with a one-unit increase in the covariate (for continuous variables) or relative to a reference group (for categorical variables).

  • Positive coef → increased hazard (i.e., higher risk)
  • Negative coef → decreased hazard (i.e., protective)
  • A coef close to 0 suggests little to no effect

🧠 Note: Log hazards are difficult to interpret directly, so we usually exponentiate them.

🔹 exp(coef)
This is the hazard ratio (HR) — the exponentiated coef, which expresses effect size more intuitively.

  • exp(coef) > 1 → Increased hazard
  • exp(coef) < 1 → Decreased hazard
  • exp(coef) = 1 → No effect

🔎 Example: exp(coef) = 1.20 means a 20% higher hazard.
exp(coef) = 0.80 means a 20% lower hazard.

🔹 se(coef)
The standard error of the coefficient — reflects how much uncertainty surrounds the estimate.

  • Smaller values = more precise estimates
  • Larger values = more uncertainty (due to small samples or noisy data)

🔹 z and Pr(>|z|)
- z is the Z-statistic, calculated as coef / se(coef)
- Pr(>|z|) is the p-value from the Wald test for each coefficient

This tests the null hypothesis that the covariate has no effect (coef = 0).

  • p < 0.05 → Statistically significant
  • p > 0.05 → Not statistically significant

📊 Model-Level Statistics

🔸 Concordance
- Measures the model’s ability to correctly rank survival times (similar to AUC for classification)
- Ranges from 0.5 (random) to 1.0 (perfect)
- In clinical data, values of 0.6–0.75 are typical

🔸 Likelihood Ratio Test
- Compares the full model to a null (intercept-only) model
- Tests overall model fit
- p < 0.05 = your model significantly improves fit

🔸 Wald Test
- Aggregates the individual Wald tests across all covariates
- Tests whether any coefficients differ significantly from 0

🔸 Score (Logrank) Test
- Rank-based test comparing observed vs expected survival
- Can be slightly more sensitive in small samples
- Often used as an overall model check

✅ In practice: Use all three global tests and the concordance to evaluate how well the model explains variation in survival. If all are non-significant, the model may lack predictive utility.

Interpreting the Cox Model Output

Understanding the coxph() output is essential for interpreting survival models. Here’s a breakdown of the most common components you’ll encounter in both univariable and multivariable Cox regressions:

🔹 coef
This is the log hazard ratio — the raw estimate from the model. It represents the change in the log hazard of the event (e.g., death) associated with a one-unit increase in the covariate (for continuous variables) or relative to a reference group (for categorical variables).

  • Positive coef → increased hazard (i.e., higher risk)
  • Negative coef → decreased hazard (i.e., protective)
  • A coef close to 0 suggests little to no effect

🧠 Note: Log hazards are difficult to interpret directly, so we usually exponentiate them.

🔹 exp(coef)
This is the hazard ratio (HR) — the exponentiated coef, which expresses effect size more intuitively.

  • exp(coef) > 1 → Increased hazard
  • exp(coef) < 1 → Decreased hazard
  • exp(coef) = 1 → No effect

🔎 Example: exp(coef) = 1.20 means a 20% higher hazard.
exp(coef) = 0.80 means a 20% lower hazard.

🔹 se(coef)
The standard error of the coefficient — reflects how much uncertainty surrounds the estimate.

  • Smaller values = more precise estimates
  • Larger values = more uncertainty (due to small samples or noisy data)

🔹 z and Pr(>|z|)
- z is the Z-statistic, calculated as coef / se(coef)
- Pr(>|z|) is the p-value from the Wald test for each coefficient

This tests the null hypothesis that the covariate has no effect (coef = 0).

  • p < 0.05 → Statistically significant
  • p > 0.05 → Not statistically significant

📊 Model-Level Statistics

🔸 Concordance
- Measures the model’s ability to correctly rank survival times (similar to AUC for classification)
- Ranges from 0.5 (random) to 1.0 (perfect)
- In clinical data, values of 0.6–0.75 are typical

🔸 Likelihood Ratio Test
- Compares the full model to a null (intercept-only) model
- Tests overall model fit
- p < 0.05 = your model significantly improves fit

🔸 Wald Test
- Aggregates the individual Wald tests across all covariates
- Tests whether any coefficients differ significantly from 0

🔸 Score (Logrank) Test
- Rank-based test comparing observed vs expected survival
- Can be slightly more sensitive in small samples
- Often used as an overall model check

✅ In practice: Use all three global tests and the concordance to evaluate how well the model explains variation in survival. If all are non-significant, the model may lack predictive utility.

Understanding Spline and Non-Linear Transformations in Survival Models

Why model transformations at all?
The Cox Proportional Hazards model assumes that each covariate has a linear relationship with the log hazard. For continuous variables like age, this means that each one-unit increase is expected to have the same multiplicative effect on the hazard — regardless of whether someone is 40 or 80 years old. This is often unrealistic.

To fix this, we apply non-linear transformations to more accurately capture the shape of the relationship between a variable (like age) and the outcome (e.g., death).

What are spline transformations?
Splines are a flexible way to model non-linear relationships without explicitly specifying the form of the curve. They break a variable into segments using predefined cut points called knots, and fit separate curves (usually polynomials) between them, ensuring smoothness at the joins.

They allow the model to “bend” as needed — making them perfect for capturing complex patterns in the data.

Common types of transformations:

• Restricted Cubic Splines (Natural Splines):
This is the most common spline in Cox models. It creates piecewise cubic polynomials between knots, but constrains the ends to be linear — which prevents overfitting at the tails.

  • ✅ Best for flexible yet stable curve fitting
  • 📌 Recommended when you suspect non-linearity but don’t want to manually test transformations

• Quadratic or Polynomial Terms (e.g., age + I(age^2)):
Adds a squared (or cubic) term to capture curvature.

  • ✅ Simple to interpret
  • ⚠️ Risky at the extremes — can overfit or behave erratically

• Log Transformations (e.g., log(age)):
Used when the effect decelerates — i.e., rapid effect early on that flattens with increasing value.

  • ✅ Great when data is skewed
  • ⚠️ Doesn’t work if the variable contains 0s or negatives

• Categorization (e.g., age groups):
Turns a continuous variable into bins (e.g., under 50, 50–65, over 65).

  • ✅ Easy to explain
  • ⚠️ Loses statistical power and may mislead if cutoffs are arbitrary

When should you use each?

Transformation Use when…
Splines (ns()) You want a smooth, data-driven relationship
Quadratic term You suspect a simple “U-shaped” or curvilinear trend
Log transform The variable increases rapidly then levels off
Categories Clinical guidelines or reporting require it

How many knots do I use for splines?
A good rule of thumb (Harrell, 2015):

  • 3 knots: small datasets (n < 100)
  • 4–5 knots: medium datasets (n ~ 100–300)
  • 5–7 knots: large datasets

Knots are often placed at percentiles of the variable — e.g., 5th, 35th, 65th, 95th percentile.

Final tip: Always re-check Schoenfeld residuals and Martingale residuals after applying any transformation.

Understanding ANOVA in Cox Regression

In the context of Cox proportional hazards models, ANOVA isn’t used in the traditional sense (like comparing means). Instead, it’s a way of comparing nested models using their log-likelihoods.

This is typically done using the Likelihood Ratio Test (LRT), which answers the question:

“Does the more complex model fit significantly better than the simpler one?”

🔍 What the Output Shows:

  • Model 1: The simpler model (fewer terms or more constrained terms)
  • Model 2: The more complex model (extra variables or flexible terms, e.g., splines)
  • Log-likelihoods: How well each model fits the data (less negative = better)
  • Chisq: Computed as 2 × (LL₂ − LL₁)
  • Degrees of freedom: Difference in number of parameters
  • p-value: Significance of improvement in fit

✅ How to Interpret:

  • p < 0.05 → The complex model fits significantly better → keep it
  • p ≥ 0.05 → No meaningful improvement → stick with the simpler model

This approach is especially useful for: - Deciding whether to model continuous variables non-linearly (e.g., with splines) - Evaluating whether new covariates add predictive value - Performing stepwise model refinement

📦 Takeaway:
Use ANOVA/LRT in Cox regression to guide decisions about model complexity vs parsimony, balancing better fit with interpretability.

How to Interpret Term Plots in Cox Models

Term plots visualize the partial effect of each covariate in a fitted model while holding other variables constant. They are especially helpful for interpreting transformations like splines.

🔹 What is a term plot?
A term plot shows how the log hazard (on the y-axis) changes with a covariate (on the x-axis), after accounting for all other variables in the model.

  • The blue line is the estimated partial effect of that variable.
  • The dashed orange lines show the 95% confidence interval.
  • The interpretation depends on the type of variable:

🔸 Continuous Variables (e.g., Age):

  • For linear terms, the blue line should be roughly straight.
  • For splines or transformed terms:
    • A curved blue line indicates a non-linear relationship.
    • Areas where the confidence band does not include 0 suggest a statistically significant effect on the log hazard.
    • Patterns like U-shapes, inflection points, or steep climbs reveal how risk evolves across the covariate range.

🔸 Binary Variables (e.g., Treatment, Prior Therapy):

  • These are shown as two horizontal segments:
    • One for each group (e.g., Control = 0, Intervention = 1).
    • The difference between the two shows the log hazard shift due to that variable.
  • If the confidence intervals include zero, there’s no strong evidence that the variable affects the hazard.

✅ Best Uses:

  • Confirming that spline-transformed terms behave as expected.
  • Checking if a linear term should be transformed (e.g., age might be better modeled with splines).
  • Double-checking whether binary variables have any meaningful effect.

🧠 Tip: Term plots don’t replace statistical tests, but they visualize model estimates clearly and intuitively.

5 Interaction or Additive Effects?

In Cox regression, interaction terms allow us to test whether the effect of one covariate on the hazard changes depending on the level of another covariate. If significant, this suggests a departure from simple additivity and may require stratified or more complex modelling.

Understanding Interaction Effects in Cox Models

In regression models—including Cox proportional hazards models—an interaction effect tests whether the relationship between a covariate and the outcome changes depending on the level of another covariate.

In practical terms, interaction terms allow us to ask:
> “Does the effect of Variable A on survival depend on Variable B?”

Why Test for Interaction?

  • To explore whether treatment effects vary across subgroups (e.g., age, gender, biomarker levels).
  • To identify non-additive relationships that a main-effects-only model would miss.
  • To refine risk stratification or support personalized approaches to care.

What It Tells Us

In the Cox model, including an interaction (e.g., treatment * age) allows the hazard ratio for treatment to vary at different values of age. If the interaction term is statistically significant, it suggests the effect of treatment is modified by age.

This can reveal: - A treatment that works well in younger patients but poorly in older ones. - A covariate that only has an impact in the presence of another factor. - Complex survival dynamics that are hidden in simpler additive models.

Interpreting Output

  • A significant interaction coefficient indicates a departure from additivity.
  • It’s essential to cautiously interpret main effects in the presence of interactions, as their meaning may be conditional.
  • Visualization (e.g., stratified survival curves or effect plots) can aid interpretation.

When to Use

  • After fitting and interpreting a main-effects model.
  • When clinical theory or prior evidence suggests effect modification.
  • When residual plots or diagnostics hint at unexplained heterogeneity.

Understanding Deviance Residuals in Cox Models

Deviance residuals are a type of diagnostic measure used to assess how well a Cox proportional hazards model fits the observed survival data. Each deviance residual reflects the discrepancy between the observed outcome (event or censoring) and what the model predicts for an individual, after accounting for all covariates.

🔍 Why we use them: - Outlier detection: Large residuals (positive or negative) may indicate subjects whose survival times were poorly predicted by the model. - Model fit check: If residuals cluster systematically or display non-random patterns when plotted against the linear predictor, it may suggest issues such as: - Mis-specification of functional form (e.g., nonlinear effects not properly captured), - Omitted interactions or variables, - Violation of model assumptions.

📊 How to interpret: - Deviance residuals are approximately normally distributed around 0. - Values far from zero may indicate poor fit for specific observations. - A balanced, symmetrical scatter around the horizontal axis suggests a well-fitting model.

🧠 When to use: - After fitting a Cox model, especially when refining the covariate structure. - In combination with martingale and Schoenfeld residuals to build a comprehensive diagnostic view. - Before finalizing your model, particularly if you are considering transformations or interactions.

💡 Bonus tip: Unlike martingale residuals, deviance residuals are symmetrically distributed and therefore better suited for identifying both under- and over-predictions, making them particularly useful for graphical diagnostics.
